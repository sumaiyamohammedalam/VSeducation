Welcome to VSedu

Your ultimate study companion app.

EduMind AI is a study tool designed to help students understand complex subjects using active recall and structured learning methods.

What is this website?

This website will eventually allow students to upload documents and generate summaries, flashcards, and quizzes.

The goal of this project is to create an AI-powered study tool that leverages active recall and structured learning methods to help students understand complex subjects more effectively.

What is VSedu?

VSedu is a free web application that helps students turn long, complex documents into clear, structured study material. It also provides many different study tools and resources to enhance learning, from various active recall techniques to exams and quizzes generated by AI.

  • Simplified notes
  • Flashcards with spaced repetition
  • Quizzes and exams
  • Personalized study plans
  • Progress tracking
  • AI-powered insights
  • Interactive learning
  • 30+ active recall methods

Research-Based Study Methods

EduMind AI is built around proven learning techniques that promote active recall, deep understanding, and long-term retention. These methods guide how notes, flashcards, and quizzes are generated.

Lecture to Ghosts

Students explain a concept out loud from memory as if teaching an invisible audience. Whenever they get stuck, it reveals gaps in understanding that need immediate review.

This method transforms passive reading into active teaching-level learning and is especially effective for computer science and physics concepts.

Whiteboard Algorithm Simulation

Learners manually simulate algorithms step by step, acting as the computer and tracking changes in memory, variables, and control flow.

This builds deep intuition for how algorithms and systems actually operate internally.

One Sheet of Truth

After studying a topic, students compress the entire concept into a single page containing only core ideas, diagrams, and formulas.

If the page becomes too crowded, it signals incomplete understanding.

Active Recall Matrix

Concepts are tested across multiple dimensions: explanation, derivation, application, and tricky edge cases.

This ensures knowledge is flexible and usable rather than memorized.

Problem Pyramid

Problems are structured from basic exercises to advanced and creative challenges, allowing students to gradually build confidence and mastery.

90/20 Deep Focus Loop

Study sessions alternate between 90 minutes of focused work and 20 minutes of intentional recovery.

This prevents mental fatigue while maintaining high cognitive performance.

Derivation from Memory

Students attempt to re-derive formulas, proofs, or algorithms without looking at references.

This reinforces understanding of structure rather than memorization.

Code Without Running

Learners write code on paper and predict output, time complexity, and memory usage before executing it.

This builds strong mental simulation skills and debugging ability.

Interleaved Practice

Instead of studying one topic for long periods, learners alternate between related subjects.

This improves abstraction and transfer of knowledge across domains.

Error Logbook

Students track mistakes, record correct reasoning, and note how to avoid similar errors in the future.

Over time, this creates a personalized database of learning insights.

Explainer Animations

Students create mental or sketched animations of how a process unfolds, such as recursion stacks, matrix transformations, or system pipelines.

Visualizing motion and structure builds intuition that static text cannot provide.

Hero Questions

Learners ask high-resolution questions that probe what is really happening beneath the surface of formulas, code, or systems.

This reveals hidden assumptions and strengthens conceptual clarity.

Pretend Office Hours

Students role-play both the confused learner and the expert instructor, asking and answering questions aloud.

Any unanswered question immediately exposes gaps that need further study.

Blank Page Test

Before studying, students write everything they remember about a topic on a blank page without references.

Repeating the test after studying highlights concrete learning gains and gaps.

Reverse Card Method

Instead of answering questions, learners generate questions for which a given concept or formula would be the correct answer.

This deepens understanding and strengthens conceptual connections.

Closed Book Problem Shuffle

Students attempt short bursts of randomly selected problems from different topics without consulting notes.

Even partial attempts strengthen recall and problem recognition.

Explain in Three Levels

Learners explain the same concept at three levels: to a beginner, to a peer, and to an expert.

This compresses understanding and exposes weaknesses in abstraction.

Predict the Next Page

Before reading further, students predict the next theorem, algorithm, or conclusion based on current information.

This activates anticipatory recall and improves retention.

Memory Stack Trace

Learners analyze their own recall process step by step, identifying where assumptions or jumps in reasoning occurred.

Treating memory like a program helps debug understanding.

Fill-in-the-Gaps Derivations

Key steps are removed from derivations or proofs, and students attempt to reconstruct them from memory.

This reinforces structural understanding and logical flow.

Speed Recall Bursts

Learners list as many related concepts as possible within a short timed interval.

This strengthens topic mapping and retrieval speed.

What-If Active Recall

Students modify assumptions or parameters and predict how outcomes would change.

This tests deep understanding and adaptability.

Draw All Diagrams from Memory

Learners recreate diagrams, system architectures, or process flows without looking at references.

Missing elements immediately reveal weak spots.

Chunk Collapse

Large topics are broken into smaller chunks, then compressed back into a single high-level explanation.

This improves synthesis and long-term retention.

Lightning Round Questions

Learners answer rapid-fire conceptual questions aloud without pausing to check notes.

This builds fluency and confidence under pressure.

Memory from Opposites

Concepts are recalled by contrasting them with their opposites, such as stack versus queue or greedy versus dynamic programming.

This sharpens conceptual boundaries and distinctions.

Derive the Exceptions

Students identify scenarios where common rules, algorithms, or formulas fail.

Understanding limitations leads to exam-level mastery.

Role-Play Debate

Learners argue opposing viewpoints about a concept or theory, defending each side with reasoning.

This strengthens critical thinking and nuance.

Time-Travel Recall

Students imagine forgetting a topic completely and rebuild it from fundamental principles.

This reinforces foundational understanding.

Error-First Recall

Learners recall previous misconceptions before reconstructing the correct understanding.

Errors become powerful anchors for long-term memory.
